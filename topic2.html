Portfolio #2: Data, Information, and Information Systems

Data and Information

Data

Data represents raw elements or unprocessed facts, from numerical values and symbolic representations to textual content and visual imagery. When collected and observed without interpretation, these elements remain mere data points—discrete and disorganized entities lacking inherent meaning or significance. (Jain, 2025).

Information

You get information when data is processed, organized, interpreted, and structured. The comprehensible output derived from raw data helps inform decisions, strategies, and actions. Information is data made valuable and accessible—an integral component of decision-making. (Jain, 2025)

Information Systems

An information system (IS) is an interconnected set of components used to collect, store, process and transmit data and digital information. At its core, it is a collection of hardware, software, data, people and processes that work together to transform raw data into useful information. An IS supports a variety of business objectives, such as improved customer service or increased operational efficiency.

Typically, an IS comprises an integrated set of hardware and software, plus databases and communications networks that facilitate data collection, storage and use. People and processes are also vital components of an IS. (Awati, 2024)

Different Types of Support Systems in Information Systems

Management Information System (MIS)

These computerized systems collect, store, process and present data to support management decision-making. Different kinds of organizations and industries use different types of MISes. For example, an MIS in a hospital may collect data on patient admissions, treatments and outcomes to help administrators make decisions about resource allocation and process improvements.

Decision Support System (DSS)

A DSS enables users to explore and analyze data to gain insights into business performance and, accordingly, make data-driven decisions. For example, a retail chain may use a DSS to collect and analyze data on customer demographics, buying behavior and sales performance in order to guide changes in inventory management and marketing campaigns. A DSS is also known as a business intelligence (BI) system.

Knowledge Work System (KWS)

These systems provide useful insights for knowledge workers, such as researchers, analysts and consultants. For example, marketing analysts may use a KWS to analyze customer data and track the performance of marketing campaigns.

Executive Information System (EIS)

An EIS provides high-level information about the company's financial performance, market trends, competitive landscape and so on. It also provides analytical tools that enable senior executives to make strategic decisions based on the available information.

Knowledge Management System (KMS)

A KMS is an information system used to update and manage the organization's knowledge resources.

Inventory Management System (IMS)

An IMS collects inventory information and generates insights to help with inventory management.

Transaction Processing System (TPS)

A TPS processes transactional data and generates useful insights to help streamline transactional processes.

Analysis and Reaction

When most people hear the word “data,” they often just think of numbers, facts, or details that have been gathered and placed into some kind of chart or document. While that’s not totally wrong, it doesn’t really capture the bigger picture, especially when we’re talking about computers and the fields connected to them. Data in computer studies isn’t just about collecting information; it’s about how that information is processed, organized, and turned into something meaningful that people can actually use. That’s where the concept of information comes in, because data on its own can feel random or incomplete, but once it’s interpreted, it becomes useful knowledge.

This lesson focuses on understanding the difference between data and information, diving into what information systems are, and exploring the various types of information systems that play a huge role in technology today. Beginning with Data and Information, data refers to raw facts like numbers, texts, symbols, or images that have no real use on their own. Once processed and organized, data becomes information, which carries meaning and value. Information is what guides decisions, strategies, and actions, making it far more useful than plain data. (Jain, 2025). This difference matters because data without context is just random, while information is what people and organizations actually depend on to solve problems and plan effectively.

Information Systems (IS) is an integrated set of components for collecting, storing and processing data. Information systems use technology and structure to collect, process, and share data, helping people and organizations work efficiently. They combine hardware, software, networks, people, and processes to ensure information is stored and managed properly. What makes them unique is that they are not only technical tools but also social systems, since people interact with the technology to keep information accurate, reliable, and meaningful for decision-making and problem-solving in different settings. These are the systems that provide productivity and interaction.

The 7 different kinds of IS (Information Systems) are as follows: Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) manage daily tasks like billing, payroll, and reservations. Management Information Systems (MIS) take TPS data and turn it into structured reports for decision-making. Decision Support Systems (DSS) go further by offering analytical tools, while Executive Support Systems (ESS) guide leaders toward long-term strategies. Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) integrates organizational processes into one system, while Supply Chain Management (SCM) improves customer relations and logistics. Knowledge Management Systems (KMS) gather and share organizational knowledge to prevent repetition and support teamwork (Awati, 2024).

Together, these systems demonstrate how information systems are vital in all industries. They shape how organizations operate, grow, and adapt to challenges, showing their importance not only in efficiency but also in maintaining competitiveness in today’s fast-changing world.
